About StoichSolve
BSED Science Research project — interactive chemistry education technology

What is StoichSolve?

StoichSolve is a web-based interactive stoichiometry platform built as a BSED Science Research project. Unlike calculators that give instant answers, StoichSolve acts as a virtual chemistry teacher — guiding every step with progressive hints, real-time feedback, and curriculum-aligned explanations based directly on the official module.

Mission

Build genuine understanding of stoichiometry through interactive guided problem solving — not just correct answers.

Research Focus

Investigating whether technology-assisted guided solving improves student confidence vs traditional methods.

Scientists Who Made This Possible

R
Jeremias Benjamin Richter (1762–1807)
German Chemist

First coined the term "stoichiometry" in 1792, defining it as the science of measuring quantitative proportions in chemical reactions.

A
Amedeo Avogadro (1776–1856)
Italian Scientist

Proposed Avogadro's Law in 1811: equal volumes of gases at same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of particles. His work led to Avogadro's number: 6.022 × 10²³.

O
Wilhelm Ostwald (1853–1932)
German Chemist

Introduced the term "mole" in chemistry — giving scientists a standard counting unit for substances.

🚀 Getting Started (Recommended Flow)

1
Read the topic lesson first. Go to Learn → Unit 1 and tap any topic. Read the definition, worked examples, and key formulas before attempting any practice.
2
Practice with a calculator. Go to Practice → Calculators. Select the calculator that matches the topic you just studied. Fill in the values and click Start Solving →.
3
Use hints if stuck. On each step of the solver, you get up to 3 progressive hints. Each hint gives a little more guidance — try to solve it before clicking the next hint.
4
Test yourself with the Assessment. Go to Assessment, pick a unit, and take the quiz. Try the timed mode for extra challenge. Review explanations on every answer.
✅ Tip: Complete all units in order — Unit 1 → Unit 2 → Unit 3 → Unit 4. Later units build on earlier concepts.

📚 How to Use the Learn Page

1
Click the Learn tab in the navigation bar.
2
The left sidebar shows 4 units. Click any unit to expand its topic list.
3
Click any topic name to load the lesson in the right panel.
4
Scroll through the lesson: definition → worked examples → key formulas → practice hints.
5
Use the "Next Topic →" button at the bottom to continue to the next lesson in sequence.
📌 All 20 lessons are based directly on the official BSED Stoichiometry module content.

🧮 How to Use the Calculators

1
Go to Practice → Calculators tab. You will see a grid of 12 calculator cards.
2
Click any calculator card to open it in the right panel.
3
Fill in the required fields (e.g. chemical formula, moles, grams). Use the Quick Example pills to auto-fill a sample problem.
4
Click Start Solving → to begin the interactive guided session.
5
On each step, type your answer into the input box and press Check Answer. ✅ = correct, ❌ = try again with a hint.
6
After 3 incorrect attempts, the correct answer is revealed with a full explanation.
💡 The 4 Stoichiometry Calculation calculators (Mole-to-Mole, Mass-to-Mass, Mass-to-Mole, Mole-to-Mass) need a balanced equation, a given substance + amount, and an unknown substance to find.

📝 How to Use the Problem Solver

1
Go to Practice → Problem Solver tab.
2
Type or paste your word problem in the text area. Example: "6.0 moles of H₂ react completely with N₂ to form NH₃. Calculate the amount of NH₃ produced in moles."
3
Click Analyze Problem →. The system will identify the problem type (Mole-to-Mole, Mass-to-Mass, etc.), display the balanced equation, and extract the given values.
4
Click Open [Type] Calculator → — the right calculator opens with all fields pre-filled automatically.
5
Click Start Solving → to begin the guided solution.
🔍 Supported input formats:
• Full word problems: "If 10 g of H₂ reacts with O₂, how many grams of H₂O form?"
• Shorthand labels: "Helium (Mass to Mole)" or "Zinc (Mole to Mass)"
• Reaction descriptions: "Nitrogen gas reacts with hydrogen gas to produce ammonia."

✅ How to Use the Assessment

1
Click the Assessment tab in the navigation bar.
2
Choose a difficulty level using the filter buttons: All / Beginner / Intermediate / Advanced.
3
Optionally enable the ⏱ Timed Mode toggle. Each question will have a 60-second countdown.
4
Click Start Quiz on any unit card to begin.
5
For multiple-choice: click an option. For fill-in: type your answer and press Submit.
6
After each answer, you'll see ✅ / ❌ plus a full explanation from the module. Click Next Question → to continue.
7
At the end, your score is shown with a performance badge. Click Retry to try again or Review Lesson to go back to the Learn page.

🌍 Where is Stoichiometry Used in Real Life?

🍳
Cooking & Baking
Recipes are proportional ratios — just like mole ratios. Scaling a recipe uses the same logic as stoichiometric calculations.
💊
Medicine / Pharmaceuticals
Drug manufacturers use stoichiometry to calculate exact amounts of reactants needed to produce a specific dose of medication.
🏭
Industrial Chemistry
The Haber process (N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃) produces fertilizer for global food production using stoichiometry to maximize ammonia yield.
🌱
Environmental Science
Calculating CO₂ emissions from fuel combustion and predicting pollution levels uses combustion stoichiometry.
🚗
Automotive Engineering
Engine fuel-air ratios are based on stoichiometric combustion — the exact ratio of fuel to oxygen for complete burning.
🔋
Battery Technology
Lithium-ion battery design uses stoichiometry to determine electrode material ratios for maximizing energy storage capacity.

🔧 Common Questions

Q: Why does the Problem Solver say "Not Detected"?
A: Make sure your problem includes a number with a unit (e.g. "5 moles" or "10 grams") and a clear question (e.g. "how many moles/grams…?"). Also works with shorthand like "Zinc (Mole to Mass)".
Q: How do I enter a chemical formula with subscripts?
A: Click the ⚗ button (bottom-right) to open the chemistry keyboard, or type normally and the calculator will accept both H2O and H₂O.
Q: The balanced equation field — what format should I use?
A: Use the → arrow (from the chemistry keyboard) between reactants and products. Example: N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃
Q: Can I use the app on my phone?
A: Yes — StoichSolve is mobile-responsive. For best experience on small screens, use landscape orientation. The text size buttons in the accessibility bar can help with readability.
